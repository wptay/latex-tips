\section{Labels}\label{sect:Labels}

\begin{enumerate}[(a)]
\item Use meaningful labels for your sections, equations, inequalities, figures, tables, and others. For example, use something like ``sect:Labels'' to label your section so that you know that this label is for a section. Later on in your text, you can then easily refer to it via something like ``\cref{sect:Labels}''. 

% \begin{Example}\label{eg:lipsum}
% \lipsum[1] 
% \end{Example}

\item\label{it:myitem} Tip: items can also be labeled! Example is this reference \ref{it:myitem}. 
\item Do not over label! For equations, tables, figures and most ``objects'', we usually label only those that we need to refer to in another part of the text. In some cases, you may also label important equations although you may not refer to these in any part of your text. This makes it easier for readers to refer to your equations. However, do this judiciously.
\item You should try to use the cleveref-forward package to reference labels. It is smart enough to know what you are referencing, e.g., \cref{sect:General}, \cref{sect:General,sect:Labels,sect:Equations}, and \cref{eq:X} all use the same cref command. Otherwise, you have put a tilde like ``Section~\ref{sect:General}'' to avoid line breaks immediately after ``Section''.
\item Refer to \cref{algo:mini}. 
\end{enumerate}

\section{Equations}\label{sect:Equations}

\begin{enumerate}[1)]
\item There are many ways to write equations, but I find that you really only need to use either the align or align* environments provided by amsmath. The environment align will have equation numbers at every line and align* has no equation numbers. See the tips on labeling to decide when to use align and align*. Examples are:
\begin{align}
X & = \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \label{eq:X} \\
& = z \nn
& = w,
\end{align}
and 
\begin{align*}
X & = \int_{a}^b f(x) \ud x \\
& = z                     \\
& = w \\
& = \norm*{\sum_{t=0}^\infty e^{t\bA}}. 
\end{align*}
%<*tag:equation>
Other examples are as follows:
\begin{align}
\Lambda_{\mathrm{SGLR}}(k,t) & =\max\left\{\Lambda(k,t),\Lambda_n(k,t)\right\}, \label{eq:Lambda_SGLR}    \\
\tau_{\mathrm{SGLR}}(b)      & =\min\{\tau(b),\tau_n(b)\}, \label{eq:tau_SGLR2}                           \\
\tau_{\mathrm{W-SGLR}}(b)    & =\min\{\widetilde{\tau}(b),\widetilde{\tau_n}(b)\}, \label{eq:tau_WSGLR2}  \\
\Phi_i                       & = \tau_{\mathrm{W-SGLR}}(b) + i^2,\ \text{for $i=1,\ldots,N$},         \nn
\tbC                         & = \onehot{\tbL}, \label{eq:tbC}                                            \\
\bx &= \bB\Herm\bSigma\bM\T\bPhi\bM\bA\T,\nn
d                            & = \KLD*{p_X}{p_{Z}}. \nonumber
\end{align}
%</tag:equation>
\textbf{Note when punctuation marks like ``,'' and ``.'' are required in the equations.} 

\item Please put \& before the binary operators like $=$, $\leq$ and $\geq$. You can see why by comparing the following example with that above. Do you see that the spacing behind $=$ is now missing? 
\begin{align*}
X = & \int_{a}^b f(x) \ud x \\
=   & z                     \\
=   & w. 
\end{align*}

\item Do not write something like
\begin{align*}
& \sum_{i=1}^n y_i              \\
& = z                           \\
& = w,                          \\
& \text{and therefore, we have} \\
& x + y = w.
\end{align*}
It should be written as follows:
\begin{align*}
& \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\
& = z              \\
& = w,
\end{align*}
and therefore, we have
\begin{align*}
& x + y = w.
\end{align*}
Alternatively, use the intertext command:
\begin{align*}
& \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\
& = z              \\
& = w,
\intertext{and therefore, we have}
& x + y = w.
\end{align*}

\item When to use \verb|\text| and \verb|\mathrm|? From tex.stackexchange: ``\verb|\text{divides}| and \verb|\mathrm{divides}| might give the same result, but they are conceptually different (and can actually be printed in different ways, depending on the math fonts used). Spaces in the argument of \verb|\mathrm| are ignored, for example. Moreover, \verb|\text| honors the font of the surrounding environment: it will print in italics in the statement of a theorem.''

You should use $\mathrm{abc}$ if abc is part of a math symbol but $\text{abc}$ if abc is text like in a conditional clause in a math environment. An example is as follows: $\mathrm{Exp}$ is in mathrm while ``if'' and ``otherwise'' are in text. 
\begin{align}
f_{\mathrm{Exp}}(x) = 
\begin{cases}
\lambda e^{-\lambda x} & \text{if}\ x > 0,\\
0 & \text{otherwise}. 
\end{cases}
\end{align}

\item Please refrain from using the \verb|$$ ... $$| or \verb|\[ ... \]| environment for equation displays. It makes the latex hard for me to read and makes it difficult to add labeling later on. Tip: If you find it a hassle to type the begin and end tags, set up some keyboard shortcuts! You should have keyboard shortcuts for the commonly used environments and commands to improve your writing efficiency. A useful tool is AutoHotkey.

\item An interesting package is breqn, which provides special equation environments that can perform line breaks \emph{automatically}. This is very useful if you need to produce both single- and double-column versions of the same paper. Do check it out! As there are still some bugs in this package, I recommend that you use the breqn environments only for those few long equations, while sticking to align and align* for the rest.

\item We define a function 
\begin{align}
\begin{aligned}
f :\ & \calH \to \Real \\
& \bx \mapsto \norm{\bx}^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{align}
Note the use of aligned environment here. 
\end{enumerate}

\section{Figures and Tables}\label{sect:Graphics}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Put your figures into a subfolder called ``Figures''.
\item You can set the graphics path in tex using \verb|\graphicspath| (this has been done in the preamble.tex file). Then \LaTeX\ will look for the right figure in the folder you have provided in the graphics path. See \cref{fig:tandem} for an example.
\item In \cref{fig:tandem}, we use the subfigure environment to create \cref{fig:network,fig:regions}.
\item I highly recommend Ipe (\url{http://ipe.otfried.org/}) for preparing your graphics. The advantage is that you can directly input latex symbols into your graphics using Ipe. Graphics can be saved directly in pdf. For example, you can open tandem.pdf in the Figures folder using the Ipe program and edit that! There is also an Ipelet that allows you to convert Ipe to tikz if you prefer the latter. Another popular graphics program with Latex capability is Inkscape~(\url{https://inkscape.org/}) with the textext extension. If you use any other graphics program, the font may not be the same as your latex document. In that case, you may need to use psfrag to replace your labels.
\item Please use ``!htb'' or ``!htbp'' as options in your figure and table environments.
\item Use the subcaption package for the subfigure environment. Do not use the subfig package as it does not work with the hyperref package. See example in \cref{fig:tandem}.
\item An example of a table is shown in \cref{tab:linkresults}. Note the use of \verb|\toprule|, \verb|\midrule|, and \verb|\bottomrule| from the booktabs package instead of the ugly \verb|\hline| command. The advice from the booktabs package is to ``Never, ever use vertical rules.'' and ``Never use double rules.'' 
\item To have fixed column widths with text wrapping in your table column, use the \verb|L{width}|, \verb|C{width}|, or \verb|R{width}| column types from the preamble.tex.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scientific Writing}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Break long sentences into multiple short sentences, especially when mathematical notations are involved.
\item Write linearly with the cause coming before the effect. E.g., instead of writing ``We have $f(x)>0$ since $x>0$,'' say ``Since $x>0$, we have $f(x)>0$.''
\item The word ``including'' means that the following list is non-exhaustive. Therefore, it is redundant and incorrect to write ``etc.'' at the end. E.g., ``GNNs have applications in many areas, including chemistry, finance, social networks, etc.'' should be written as ``GNNs have applications in many areas, including chemistry, finance, and social networks.''
\item Do not start a sentence with a mathematical symbol or citation number. Use ``The paper [1] proposes \dots'' or ``The matrix $\bA$ is positive definite \dots''.
\item Know when you need to use commas after Latin abbreviations. We write ``e.g., a dog'' and ``i.e., it is red'' but ``cf.\ Section 3'' and ``Sun et al.\ proved Theorem 1''.
\item Refrain from starting a sentence with ``and''. This is informal language and appears in formal writing only very rarely to emphasize the connection between two sentences. E.g., ``We have proven that nitrogen cures cancers. And it is widely available.''
\item Try not to use phrases like ``easy to show'' and ``it is obvious that''. Instead, say ``it can be shown that'' after satisfying yourself that it indeed can be shown. Historically, some ``obvious'' things have proven to be very difficult to show rigorously.  
\item We write $x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n$ and $(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$. Note the difference between $\cdots$ and $\ldots$. You can also use $\dots$ from the amsmath package if unsure which to use.
\item Please write in formal language and refrain from using flowery words. Do not use short forms, e.g., ``it's'' should be written as ``it is'', ``we've'' should be written as ``we have'', and ``thanks very much for your comment'' should be rephrased as ``thank you for your comment''. Avoid descriptions such as ``innovative'' and ``pioneering'' when describing your own work. These are judgments to be made by other researchers years later.
\item Write in the \textbf{active present tense}. E.g., 
\begin{itemize}
\item ``In this paper, we \cyan{shall show} that $A=B$.'' reads better as ``In this paper, we \cyan{show} that $A=B$.''
\item  ``In our experiments, we \cyan{divided} the data into training and testing sets.'' reads better as ``In our experiments, we \cyan{divide} the data into training and testing sets.''
\item ``In \cite{kipf2016semi}, the authors \cyan{used} a GCN model to classify the nodes.'' reads better as ``In \cite{kipf2016semi}, the authors \cyan{use} a GCN model to classify the nodes.''
\end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Paper Editing Process}\label{sect:editing}
\begin{enumerate}
\item After you have written your paper, put it aside for a day and revise it with a fresh mind in order to improve it. \textbf{This includes checking the bibliography for errors.} See \cref{sect:Bibliography}.
\item Please send me your paper for editing only after you have gone over it \textbf{several times} to correct any errors and improve it. You should send the paper to me only after you feel fully satisfied with the version that you have. Typically, this will be the fourth or fifth version.

\begin{Example}\label{eg:subGSP}
Consider the subset of vertices $V_0$ of an unweighted directed graph $G$. Suppose that we consider the adjacency matrix $\bA_G$ as the graph shift operator for $G$. The adjacency matrix $\bA_{H_0}$ of the subgraph $H_0$ shown is a submatrix consisting of the even-indexed rows and columns of $\bA_G^2$. Any filter shift invariant \gls{wrt} $\bA_{H_0}$ can be viewed as a polynomial of $\bA_G^2$ composed with a projection to the vertices of $H_0$. See \cite{kipf2016semi} for more details.
\end{Example}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:limit_var}}\label[Appendix]{proof:thm:limit_var}

We have 
\begin{align}\label{eq:cov_operators}
	\begin{aligned}
		\bC_{\bz\bz}:L^2(\calJ_S)&\to L^2(\calJ_S)\\
		g(\cdot)&\mapsto
		%\int_{\calJ_S} k(\cdot,\bxi)g(\bxi) \ud \zeta(\bxi)\\
		\int_{\calT} \sum_{u\in\set{v_0}\setcomp} k_G(v,u)k_\calT(\bt,\bs)g(u,\bs)\ud \tau(\bs),\\
		\bC_{\bz \bx_0}:L^2(\calT_0)&\to L^2(\calJ_S) \\
		g(\cdot)&\mapsto \int_{\calT_0}k_G(v_0,v)k_\calT(\bt,\bs)g(v_0,\bs) \ud \tau(\bs).
	\end{aligned}
\end{align}
The proof is now complete.
